UX Case Study · Product Design

Why users weren't booking — and how a smaller payment ask fixed it

FlyDining is a franchise-based dining platform. Customers were browsing events, selecting seats, then abandoning at payment. This case study covers how I found the root cause — a commitment model problem, not a UX problem — and built a solution that increased advance bookings.

Why this mattered

Low bookings didn't just hurt revenue — they made operators lose faith in the digital channel. Less faith = fewer events listed = platform stalls. It was a flywheel problem.

02 Problem Framing

The real problem wasn't the UI — it was the commitment model

My first instinct was to improve the page — better layout, clearer copy, more social proof. That would have been the wrong call.

That's when I realised: users were completing every step right up to payment. If they weren't interested, they'd leave at browsing. They were leaving after choosing a seat. The wall wasn't the design. It was the ask.

Reframed Problem

We were asking first-time users to pay the full amount — non-refundable — for a venue they'd never visited. That's not a layout problem. It's a commitment model problem.

04 Core Insight

Intent was there. We were pricing it out.

"Users weren't abandoning because they didn't want to attend. They were abandoning because we asked them to fully commit before we'd given them a reason to trust us."

We couldn't fix trust overnight — that takes time and reputation. But we could remove the commitment barrier immediately. That's when I realised: the solution wasn't a redesign. It was a different model.

05 Hypothesis

If we reduce the initial ask, more users will commit

Design Decision

Simple bet: let users pay 20% upfront to hold their slot. Collect the rest at the venue. Same revenue — different timing. No all-or-nothing risk.

Before

Full payment upfront

High financial risk. Non-refundable. Rational response: abandon.

After

20% advance to reserve

Low risk. Slot confirmed. Balance at arrival. Risk proportional to certainty.

Hypothesis

Lower the initial ask → more users complete bookings → total revenue unchanged. The intent was already there.

06 Validation

So I tested it manually before building anything

Experiment (Pilot Test)

Before writing a line of code, I wanted real signal. So we ran a manual pilot — four steps, no product changes.

1

Pull abandoners from backend logs

Users who reached payment and left — high intent, no conversion. Exactly who we needed.

2

Operators reached out directly

"We noticed you were interested — can we help?" Simulated what an automated trigger would eventually do in-product.

3

Offered 20% advance — off-platform

"Reserve now with 20%, pay the rest when you arrive." No product changes. Testing the concept, not the UI.

✓

Abandoners converted

Users who had previously walked away followed through. Hypothesis confirmed.

10 Trade-offs & Risks

What we accepted — and why

Shipping faster meant accepting some open questions. These weren't mistakes — they were deliberate calls.

Risk

No-shows. A 20% advance is low enough that some users may not show up. Needed: clear forfeiture policy and a cancellation window close to the event date.

Risk

Cash flow shift. Operators receive less before the event. Required transparent communication and an updated payout model with franchise partners.

Trade-off

Manual balance collection in v1. The 80% is collected at the venue by staff. We accepted this to ship fast — digital collection is the follow-on.

Trade-off

Advance option shown first. May nudge high-intent users away from full payment. Chosen intentionally — but needs monitoring over time.

11 What I'd Do Next

Three clear follow-ons

1

Automate cancellation and no-show logic

Currently handled operationally. Needs to be product-enforced — policies surfaced to users at booking time — so it scales without operator intervention.

2

Test dynamic advance thresholds

20% was a starting point from pilot data. Does 15% convert more? Does 30% reduce no-shows? The right amount likely varies by event type.

3

Address the trust barrier

The advance option solved commitment. But trust is still unresolved. Reviews, operator profiles, past event photos — that's the next layer of the problem.

The problem was never that users didn't want to book.

It was that we built a checkout assuming they already trusted us — before we'd given them a reason to. Once we changed the model to match the actual level of trust users had at that stage, the behaviour changed too.
