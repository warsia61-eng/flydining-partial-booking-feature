UX Case Study · Product Design

Why users weren't booking — and how a smaller payment ask fixed it

FlyDining is a franchise-based dining platform. Customers were browsing events, selecting seats, then abandoning at payment. This case study covers how I found the root cause — a commitment model problem, not a UX problem — and built a solution that increased advance bookings.

01 Context

How FlyDining works — and why bookings matter so much

FlyDining lets independent franchise operators list exclusive dining experiences on a shared digital platform. Customers discover events, pick dates, and book seats online.

But unlike a regular restaurant reservation, bookings here are critical for operations. Operators use confirmed headcounts to plan staffing, buy ingredients, and prepare logistics. Without reliable numbers ahead of an event, they can't plan — and if they can't plan, they stop listing events. The platform's entire growth depends on the checkout working.

Why this mattered

This wasn't just a conversion rate problem. Unpredictable booking volume was causing operators to lose confidence in the digital channel — which threatened the entire franchise growth model.

02 Problem Framing

The real problem wasn't the UI — it was the commitment model

When I first looked at this problem, the instinct was to improve the booking page: better layout, clearer copy, more social proof. That would have been the wrong call.

To understand what was actually happening, I traced the problem from the business goal down to user behavior. The key distinction: if users weren't interested, they'd leave while browsing. But they were leaving at payment — after selecting a date and choosing seats. Intent was clearly there. The payment step was the wall.

Reframed Problem

We were asking first-time users to pay the full amount — non-refundable — for a venue they'd never visited and an experience they couldn't verify. That's not a layout problem. It's a trust and commitment model problem.

03 Research & Observations

Three signals that pointed to the same root cause

I looked at the problem from three angles: operator feedback, user behavior, and analytics. Each gave me a piece of the picture — and they all converged on the same answer.

1

Operators said booking volume was unpredictable

Franchise partners reported they couldn't rely on the platform's numbers to plan events. They were sometimes getting far fewer guests than expected. This told me confidence in the digital booking channel was low — but I didn't yet know why.

2

Users were calling venues before booking

Several operators mentioned that customers would call them directly before committing online. This was a clear signal: users wanted to reduce uncertainty before paying. They weren't comfortable going from "discovery" to "full payment" without some reassurance first.

!

Analytics confirmed: abandonment was concentrated at payment

When I looked at the funnel data, users browsed, engaged, and moved through the flow — then dropped off at the payment screen at a disproportionately high rate. The problem wasn't awareness or interest. The friction was specific: the moment of financial commitment.

What I found: two barriers hitting users at the same time

🔒
Trust barrier

Unfamiliar venue. No prior experience. No strong social proof.

+
💳
Commitment barrier

Full amount upfront. Non-refundable. No fallback if disappointed.

Result: walking away felt like the rational, lower-risk choice

Either barrier alone might be manageable. Together, they made the cost of being wrong feel too high — especially for a first-time user with no reference point for the experience quality.

04 Core Insight

Intent was there. We were pricing it out.

"Users weren't abandoning because they didn't want to attend. They were abandoning because we asked them to fully commit before we'd given them a reason to trust us."

We couldn't fix the trust barrier quickly — trust is built through experience and reputation over time. But we could remove the commitment barrier immediately. If users could hold their slot with a smaller amount, they'd cross the threshold. The rest of the payment could be collected at the venue, once they'd actually shown up.

This reframe was important: the solution didn't need to be a redesign. It needed to be a different model.

05 Hypothesis

If we reduce the initial ask, more users will commit

The bet was straightforward: offer users a 20% advance payment option to reserve their slot, with the remaining 80% collected at the venue on arrival. This removes the all-or-nothing financial risk without sacrificing revenue — we'd still collect the full amount, just at a different point in the journey.

Before

Full payment upfront

High financial risk. Non-refundable. If the experience disappoints, the user has already lost everything. Rational response: abandon.

After

20% advance to reserve

Low financial risk. Slot confirmed immediately. Balance due at arrival. Risk is proportional to certainty.

Hypothesis

Reducing the initial payment commitment will increase booking completion without reducing total revenue — because the intent to attend was already there. We just needed to make it easier to act on.

06 Validation

We tested manually before building anything

Before investing engineering time, I wanted to know: will this actually work? So instead of shipping a feature, we ran a manual pilot. The idea was simple — simulate what the product would do, using people instead of code.

1

Identify abandoners from backend logs

We used existing server data to pull a list of users who had added a booking but left before completing payment. These were exactly the people we needed — users with demonstrated intent who hadn't converted.

2

Operators reached out directly

Franchise operators contacted these users via phone or message: "We noticed you were looking at our event — can we help?" This step simulated what an automated re-engagement trigger would eventually do in the product.

3

Offer the 20% advance option manually

When users responded, operators offered the advance payment off-platform — "reserve now with 20%, pay the rest when you arrive." No product changes required. We were testing the concept, not the interface.

✓

Compare conversion rates against the standard flow

Result: abandoners converted. When given the option to pay less upfront, users who had previously walked away followed through and confirmed their bookings. Hypothesis confirmed. Time to build.

Pilot Outcome

The barrier was the commitment model — not the product, not the design, not the copy. Users returned when the ask got smaller. This gave us a clear, evidence-backed case to build the feature.

07 Solution

A flexible payment model built into checkout

The pilot became a product feature. At the payment step, users now see two clear options: pay in full for an instant, fully-settled confirmation — or pay 20% now and settle the balance at the venue. Both paths confirm the slot immediately.

Three deliberate decisions shaped the implementation:

Decision 1

Advance option shown first

Anchors users to the lower-commitment path, reducing perceived risk before they see the full amount.

Decision 2

Slot confirms immediately

Even on the 20% path, reservation is instant. Removes fear that a partial payment leaves the booking uncertain.

Decision 3

Trust signals at decision moment

Secure payment, encrypted, slot guaranteed — visible right when the user decides. Directly addresses the trust barrier.

10 Trade-offs & Risks

What we accepted — and why we accepted it

Shipping v1 of this feature meant making some deliberate trade-offs. These weren't oversights — they were known decisions made to ship faster and learn from real usage.

Risk

No-shows on advance bookings. A 20% advance is low enough that some users may not show up. We needed clear operator policy on forfeiture, and a defined cancellation window close to the event date, to protect event economics without penalising genuine cancellations.

Risk

Cash flow timing changes for operators. Collecting most of the payment at arrival means operators receive less before the event. This required transparent communication with franchise partners and adjustments to the payout model.

Trade-off

Balance collection is manual in v1. The remaining 80% is collected at the venue by staff, creating an operational dependency. We accepted this to ship faster — with digital collection as a planned follow-on.

Trade-off

Full-payment users may be under-served. Showing the advance option first could nudge high-intent users away from full payment. We chose this intentionally to maximise completion among hesitant users — but it needs monitoring over time.

11 What I'd Do Next

Three clear follow-ons from this release

1

Automate cancellation and no-show logic

Right now, forfeiture and cancellation windows are handled operationally. This needs to be product-enforced — with clear policies surfaced to users at booking time — so it scales without depending on individual operators to manage it case-by-case.

2

Test dynamic advance thresholds

20% was chosen based on pilot conversion data. But the right amount might vary by event type, price point, or operator. A lower threshold might convert more users; a higher one might reduce no-shows. Worth testing systematically.

3

Start addressing the trust barrier

The advance option mitigated the commitment barrier. But the trust barrier is still there. Reviews, operator profiles, photos from past events, and social proof are the next layer of the conversion problem — and they compound with the payment flexibility we've already built.

The problem was never that users didn't want to book.

It was that we built a checkout assuming they already trusted us — before we'd given them a reason to. Once we changed the model to match the actual level of trust users had at that stage, the behaviour changed too.
